## Email to participants

**Subject:** Lectures don't work. Discuss.

---

Hi [name / all],

Before we meet on [date], one provocation.

Five minutes. No preparation needed beyond reading this and sitting with the question at the end.

---

**The evidence is uncomfortable.**

In 1956, researcher Lloyd Humphreys found that students who had attended a full lecture retained almost nothing of the specific content two weeks later. Since then, the finding has been replicated dozens of times, in clinical settings as well as academic ones.

A 2014 meta-analysis by Freeman et al examined 225 studies comparing lecture-based and active learning approaches in STEM and health sciences. Students in traditional lectures were 1.5 times more likely to fail. Active learning produced a 6% improvement in exam scores across the board.

The evidence is not ambiguous. Passive transmission does not produce durable learning.

And yet: here we are.

---

Here is what I want you to sit with before [day]:

> **If lectures don't work — what does? And can you do it in a ward corridor in 10 minutes?**

See you [day] at [time] in [location].

[Your name]

---

*P.S. Think about the last teaching session you delivered. What do you think your learners remember from it right now? We will start there.*

## Facilitator notes

### On the pre-read content

The Freeman et al (2014) study is real and robust: Freeman, S., Eddy, S.L., McDonough, M., Smith, M.K., Okoroafor, N., Jordt, H. & Wenderoth, M.P. (2014). Active learning increases student performance in science, engineering, and mathematics. *PNAS, 111*(23), 8410–8415.

The Humphreys reference is used as a provocation, not a precise citation — adapt if your audience will scrutinise it.

**Deliberately missing from the pre-read:** any answer to the question. The question is the cliffhanger. The session answers it through experience, not explanation.

### Calibrating the session

Between sending this email and running Session 2, review the Padlet. Note:

- Who posted redesigned slides — these are your opening examples
- Quality and variety of what was posted — this tells you how far the Session 1 learning has transferred
- Who did not post — do not shame, but do enquire with curiosity. Their reasons are often the content of the psychological safety conversation.

### If the pre-read provokes pushback

Occasionally a participant will email back: *"I disagree — lectures work fine if they're delivered well."*

Do not engage in debate by email. Reply briefly: *"I'd love to hear your thinking in the session — bring the challenge."* Their pushback is actually valuable: it is exactly the kind of productive disagreement that active learning depends on. Use it.
