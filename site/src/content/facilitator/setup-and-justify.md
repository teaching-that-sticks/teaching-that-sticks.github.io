## Bottom line up front

**Three 90-minute sessions. No licence fee. One willing facilitator. Protected time is the only non-negotiable.**

Realistic aim: clinical educators who leave with specific tools, a structured habit of applying evidence to session design, and a method for checking whether any of it worked. This is a foundation, not a transformation promise — but a foundation most NHS educators were never given.

The cost base for this programme is £0. The threshold for benefit to exceed cost is correspondingly low: a single educator who redesigns one session, retains one more concept, or changes one default returns more than was spent.

This is not a nice-to-have CPD event. It is a direct response to a documented national crisis in educator capability, a regulatory expectation for registered staff, and an investment that compounds — because better educators produce better learners, and some of those learners become educators.

## Why this is urgent

### The workforce is at risk

The NHS Educator Workforce Strategy (NHSE, 2023) states plainly: **"Without educators, we do not have a future workforce."** The same strategy reports:
- **52% of medical trainers** are at moderate or high risk of burnout
- **30% of recognised trainers** cannot use their designated educational hours because clinical service demand takes over
- There is an **"inextricable link"** between high-quality educator support and the ability to recruit, retain, and maximise productivity across the entire workforce

The 10 Year Health Plan for England (2025) describes the NHS as being at **"an existential brink"** and commits to an overhaul of education and training curricula within three years to produce a workforce "fit for the future."

### The pedagogical gap is documented — and named

NHS England's own analysis identifies a pattern: clinical staff transition to educational roles based purely on clinical seniority, creating **"unconscious incompetence" regarding evidence-based teaching methodologies**. Expertise in a speciality is not the same as expertise in teaching it. The two are different skills. Most NHS educators have only been given one of them.

### Poor teaching has a patient safety cost

A cross-sectional study (2024) found a statistically significant correlation (p < 0.001) between educator quality and the patient safety competencies of the nurses they trained. The line from educator training to patient outcome is not theoretical — it is measurable.

### The risk of doing nothing

Doing nothing is not a neutral position. The current default — a teaching session assembled in PowerPoint, delivered once, ticked off a spreadsheet, forgotten by Friday — has a cost of its own:

- It consumes the same protected time while producing a fraction of the learning
- It creates false assurance: CPD is recorded, the box is ticked, the gap remains
- It compounds over time: educators who were never taught how to teach become the tacit benchmark for those who follow them
- The regulatory expectation that registered staff receive ongoing educator development is not met by event attendance alone

A single exposure to information does not produce behaviour change. We know this. The evidence is not contested. The question for any organisation is whether it continues to spend time on the version of education that doesn't work, or begins building towards the version that does.

The pattern looks like this in operational terms:

| Educational default | Operational consequence |
|---------------------|-------------------------|
| Slides and talking | Low retention; learning does not transfer to practice |
| Single passive exposure | Behaviour unchanged; the same competency gaps recur |
| No educator development | Variable quality; reputational and safety risk |
| Information overload | Repeated training demand; time wasted re-covering ground |
| Compliance mentality | Tick-box completion; workforce capability overstated on paper |
| No evaluation beyond reaction | No improvement intelligence; problems persist undetected |

This is not a critique of individuals. Most clinical educators were never taught how to teach — they inherited presentation-based approaches because those approaches were operationally efficient and nobody questioned them. The gap is structural, not personal.

## What it is

Teaching That Sticks is a three-session CPD programme for clinical educators across all professions. Each session is 90 minutes, spaced at least one week apart, for groups of 8–20.

| Session | Title | Core question |
|---------|-------|---------------|
| 1 | Death by PowerPoint? | Why does my teaching produce so little retention? |
| 2 | Active Beats Passive | Why does knowing the evidence not change what I do? |
| 3 | Did It Stick? | How do I know if the teaching actually changed practice? |

The programme is openly licensed (CC BY 4.0). You are not purchasing access — you are commissioning delivery time.

---

## The regulatory mandate

For registered staff, educator development is not optional CPD — it is a regulatory expectation:

- **HCPC Standards of Education and Training:** providers must have effective programmes for the *"continuing professional and academic development of educators"*
- **NMC Standards Framework:** those who support and assess students must be *"suitably qualified, prepared and skilled"* and receive ongoing training
- **Nursing and Midwifery Educator Framework:** all educators, regardless of setting, are expected to employ *"andragogy and pedagogy relevant to context"*

## What it returns

Because this programme carries no licence cost, the calculation is unusual: **you only need any positive effect to achieve a return on investment.** The question is not whether the return justifies the expense — it is whether three sessions of protected time produces a different outcome than three sessions of the current default.

On the specific metrics that matter for workforce sustainability:

- Replacing a single registered nurse costs approximately **£40,000** in total; structured preceptorship and educator support are among the most evidence-based retention interventions available
- Nursing graduates in environments with high preceptor support have an **86% first-year retention rate** versus **80%** in low-support environments — a difference that compounds across a workforce

This programme does not have its own Level 4 evidence base yet — it is too new. What it is built on is an established, peer-reviewed body of evidence about how people actually learn and retain information: spaced practice, retrieval, dual-coding, active processing. These are not disputed. The question is whether your educators have been given access to them.

This programme does not require you to measure at Level 4 immediately. It does ask you to commit to Level 3 measurement: did the teaching practice of your participants change?

## Evaluation framework

The programme is built around Kirkpatrick's four-level model, applied to itself:

| Level | What it measures | How |
|-------|-----------------|-----|
| 1 — Reaction | Did participants find it valuable? | Padlet prompt at end of each session |
| 2 — Learning | Can they apply the principles? | Retrieval quiz at Session 3 opening |
| 3 — Behaviour | Did their teaching practice change? | 30/60/90-day follow-up (facilitator-led) |
| 4 — Results | Did their learners learn more effectively? | Local clinical education data — optional |

The minimum expectation is Level 3 measurement at 90 days. A simple three-question follow-up ("What changed? What got in the way? What support would help?") is sufficient.
