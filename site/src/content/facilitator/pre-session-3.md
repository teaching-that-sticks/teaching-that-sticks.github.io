*Use any clear visual representation of the forgetting curve — widely available under open licences. The x-axis shows time (hours to days), the y-axis shows retention percentage. The curve drops sharply within 24 hours, then more gradually. No caption needed. The image does the work.*

---

This is Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve, first described in 1885. The pattern has been replicated in every learning context tested since — including clinical training.

After one hour, we retain roughly 50% of new information. After one day, around 30%. After a week, less than 10% — unless something has happened to interrupt the forgetting.

Here is the question I want you to sit with before [day]:

> **Think of something you learned in a training session six months ago. What do you remember? What changed in your practice as a result?**

No need to write it down. Just be honest with yourself.

See you [day] at [time] in [location].

[Your name]

## Facilitator notes

### On the pre-read content

The Ebbinghaus curve needs no explanation in the email — the image is self-explanatory enough to create the question, which is the point. Do not explain it. The explanation happens in Session 3 when the curve returns as a named reference in the didactic input.

### Calibrating the word cloud at minute 8

The word cloud question in Session 3 — *"What changed in your practice since Session 1?"* — is the most honest feedback mechanism in the programme. The pre-read primes participants to think about this in advance. It also reduces the likelihood of blanket *"nothing"* responses by prompting genuine reflection beforehand.

However: some *"nothing"* responses will remain. Prepare for this. Review your advocacy-inquiry phrasing before the session. The pre-planned response is:

> *"Tell me more about that — what got in the way?"*

And if the answer is *"I was exhausted and working extra shifts,"* the correct response is:
> *"That makes complete sense. Thank you for saying it. That is exactly what we are going to talk about today."*

### On the retrieval quiz

Finalise your five questions before sending this email. They should draw from both Session 1 and Session 2 content — this is spaced retrieval across the full programme, not just the most recent session. See the Session 3 blueprint for suggested questions.

### On tone

This is the final pre-read. The tone should be slightly more reflective and weightier than the previous two — less punchy provocation, more invitation to honest self-assessment. The programme has earned some gravitas by now.
