## Learning outcomes

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Distinguish between passive and active learning approaches and explain the evidence base for active learning
2. Design a short active learning sequence for their own clinical context
3. Use at least two technology-enhanced learning tools in a low-risk environment
4. Name the conditions required for active learning to succeed, including psychological safety

---

### Room setup

Change the layout from Session 1. This signals that the environment is intentional. Suggest: clusters of four, no fixed "front." If possible, rearrange so the facilitator has no obvious default position.

**No slides on screen when participants arrive.** The facilitator is in the room, central, not at a lectern. This is deliberate and models the content of the session.

### Tech prep

- Mentimeter word cloud ready, question loaded: *"What did your learners remember from your last teaching session?"* — not yet launched
- Padlet from Session 1 open and visible on screen when participants enter (they should see their own commitments from last time)
- Two TEL tools chosen for the playground activity (minutes 57–67). Suggestions: Mentimeter for a poll + Padlet for a collaborative board, OR Poll Everywhere + a live quiz tool such as Kahoot. Have both open, tested, and ready.

### Jigsaw activity cards

Print four sets. Each card covers one concept from *Make It Stick* (Brown et al, 2014):

**Card A — Retrieval practice**
> Testing yourself on material is more effective than re-reading it. Every time you retrieve information, you strengthen the memory trace and increase long-term retention. Low-stakes quizzes are more powerful teaching tools than revision sessions.

**Card B — Spaced practice**
> Distributing practice over time beats massed practice (cramming). Revisiting material after a gap — even when it feels hard to recall — dramatically improves long-term retention. This is why Sessions 1, 2 and 3 are spaced weeks apart and open with retrieval.

**Card C — Interleaving**
> Mixing topics or types of problem during practice — rather than blocking one topic at a time — improves long-term learning even though it feels harder and slower in the moment. The difficulty is desirable: it is a sign of deeper processing.

**Card D — Elaborative interrogation**
> Asking *why* and *how* questions about new material, and connecting it to what you already know, produces deeper encoding than passive re-reading. Explaining something in your own words forces you to identify what you do and do not understand.

## Session flow

---

### 0–8 min | Spaced retrieval opening: what did you redesign?

No introduction. No slides. Facilitator moves to the centre of the room.

Say:
> *"Session 1 — you made a commitment. Show me."*

Pull up the Padlet. Call out names or ask for volunteers. Take three or four examples. For each: *"Talk us through what you changed and why."*

> **Facilitator note:** This is not a performance review — it is spaced retrieval, live, and you should name it as such in a moment. If someone did not complete their commitment, do not shame them. Ask: *"What got in the way?"* — and note the answer, as it may be relevant to the psychological safety conversation later.

After the examples, name what just happened:
> *"That is spaced retrieval. We have just used a core principle from cognitive science — revisiting material after a gap strengthens the memory trace. We will come back to the evidence for that in about 20 minutes."*

**Pedagogy modelled:** Spaced retrieval enacted and named, accountability, continuity across sessions.

---

### 8–16 min | Forgetting curve — experienced before explained

Launch the word cloud:
> *"Answer honestly. Think about the last time you delivered a teaching session — a ward round, a study day, a mandatory training. What do you think your learners remembered one week later?"*

Give 90 seconds for responses. Display the word cloud live. The answers will typically be sparse, uncertain, or unexpectedly negative.

Sit with the result:
> *"Look at that. What does it tell us?"*

Take two or three responses. Do not explain yet. Write *"Ebbinghaus?"* on the flipchart.

> **Facilitator note:** The goal here is to create productive discomfort before providing the theoretical framing. Clinicians who are honest about this moment — *"I have no idea what they remembered"* — are already doing better epistemically than those who assume their session landed. Hold the space without rushing to resolution.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Experience precedes theory (Kolb), Ebbinghaus forgetting curve experienced before named.

---

### 16–32 min | Jigsaw activity: Make It Stick

**Setup:** Four groups of approximately equal size. Give each group one jigsaw card (Retrieval, Spaced, Interleaving, Elaboration).

**Round 1 — become the expert (8 minutes):**
> *"Read your card. Discuss it in your group. Make sure everyone understands it well enough to teach it to someone who has never read it. Come up with one clinical example."*

**Round 2 — teach each other (8 minutes):**
> *"Regroup: form new groups of four, one person from each original group in each new group. Teach each other your concept in two minutes each."*

At the end, brief whole-group check: *"Which concept do you think is hardest to implement in a clinical environment? And why?"*

> **Facilitator note:** The jigsaw is doing multiple things simultaneously: peer teaching (which deepens the teacher's own understanding more than passive listening), assessment as learning (explaining forces gap-identification), and it models exactly the kind of active learning sequence the session is about. Name all of this afterwards — *"What you just did is what the research says works."*

**Pedagogy modelled:** Peer teaching, constructivism, assessment as learning, jigsaw as active learning structure.

---

### 32–42 min | Didactic input: active learning and TEL

**12 minutes maximum. Timer visible.**

#### Active learning spectrum — 5 minutes

Not a binary (active vs passive) but a continuum. Roughly:

*Listening → Reading → Watching → Discussing → Doing → Teaching*

The further right, the deeper the encoding — but context and safety determine what is possible. A 6 am ward handover is not the same as a protected study day.

Key distinction:
- **Assessment *of* learning** — measuring what was learned (summative)
- **Assessment *for* learning** — using assessment to guide next steps (formative)
- **Assessment *as* learning** — the act of assessment is itself the learning (jigsaw, think-pair-share, retrieval quiz)

Reference: Angelo & Cross (1993), Kolb's experiential cycle (1984).

#### TEL tools — 7 minutes

Name and briefly demonstrate three tools (not a tutorial — just enough for participants to know they exist and are accessible):

| Tool | Best use in clinical teaching |
|------|-------------------------------|
| Mentimeter / Slido | Live polls, word clouds, Q&A — low-risk participation, especially in hierarchical environments |
| Padlet | Asynchronous collaborative boards — commitment walls, shared resources, reflections between sessions |
| Poll Everywhere | Similar to Mentimeter; some NHS trusts have existing licences |
| Kahoot / Quizlet Live | Gamified retrieval practice — works well for ward teaching if tone is right |
| Microsoft Forms / Google Forms | Simple, zero-cost Kirkpatrick Level 1 feedback — everyone already has access |

> **Facilitator note:** Do not tech-evangelise. The point is that these are *options* that lower the barrier to active learning, not requirements. A well-run think-pair-share with no tech at all is more powerful than a poorly-facilitated Kahoot session.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Active learning spectrum named, TEL demonstrated by use (you have already used word cloud tools twice today).

---

### 42–57 min | Design challenge: teaching in the real world

**Brief, read aloud:**
> *"You have 12 minutes. In your group, design a teaching moment that meets all of these constraints:*
> *— 10 minutes maximum*
> *— 6 learners (mixed experience)*
> *— Ward corridor, no projector, no whiteboard*
> *— One specific learning outcome*
> *— Grounded in at least one principle from today or Session 1*
>
> *You are not designing a lecture. You are designing a learning experience."*

Give groups 10 minutes to design, then two minutes each to present back.

After all groups have presented, group votes (by show of hands or Mentimeter): *"Which design would you be most confident your learners would remember something from in a week? Why?"*

> **Facilitator note:** The ward corridor constraint is deliberate. It forces participants out of slide-deck thinking and into situated learning (Lave & Wenger). The real clinical environment is not a barrier to good teaching — it is the learning environment. Name this after the activity: *"What you just designed is what Lave and Wenger call situated cognition. The ward isn't where you apply the classroom learning. It IS the learning environment."*

**Pedagogy modelled:** Situated learning (Lave & Wenger), creativity under authentic constraint, learning transfer.

---

### 57–67 min | TEL playground

Participants try two tools themselves — not as observers, as users.

**Tool 1 (5 minutes):** Facilitator posts a question or scenario to a Mentimeter word cloud or poll. Participants respond from their own device. Whole group sees the aggregate result live. Discuss: *"How could you use this in your next teaching session?"*

**Tool 2 (5 minutes):** Participants post one thought, question, or resource to the shared Padlet. Could be: *"One active learning idea I could try this week."* Brief discussion.

> **Facilitator note:** The point is not the tools — it is the experience of being an *active participant* rather than a passive audience. Ask: *"How did that feel, compared to being talked at?"* The answer is the lesson.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Experiential learning (Kolb), TEL as participation tool not broadcast tool.

---

### 67–77 min | Psychological safety: what actually stops us?

This is the most important conversation in the programme. Do not rush it.

Ask:
> *"We've spent this session designing active, engaging, evidence-based teaching. So why isn't this what happens by default? What actually gets in the way?"*

Take answers without judgment. Common responses: time pressure, hierarchy, fear of looking incompetent, exhaustion, *"people just want the facts,"* mandatory training culture, unsupportive management.

Name it:
> *"Most of what you've just described comes back to one concept: psychological safety. Amy Edmondson at Harvard (1999) defines it as the belief that you will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up, taking risks, or making mistakes. In a hierarchically structured clinical environment, psychological safety is the precondition for active learning — not a nice-to-have. If learners do not feel safe to say 'I don't know,' to get things wrong in front of colleagues, or to challenge assumptions, all of the techniques we have discussed today become unavailable."*

Then:
> *"The same applies to you as a facilitator. Designing and running active learning requires you to give up control of the room. That takes its own kind of courage."*

Sit with this. Do not try to solve it. Ask: *"What is one small thing you could do to increase psychological safety in your next teaching session?"* Take three or four responses.

> **Facilitator note:** This conversation will surface important things — sometimes disclosures about workplace culture, bullying dynamics, or exhaustion. Hold it carefully. You are not a therapist, but you can acknowledge difficulty without dismissing it. *"What you're describing is real, and it matters"* is enough. Session 3 will return to this, particularly around the hierarchy of needs.
>
> **The NWKM link:** The New World Kirkpatrick Model (2016) identifies psychological safety as a primary *Required Driver* — the post-training environmental condition that determines whether Level 2 learning (acquiring a skill in the room) ever becomes Level 3 behaviour change (applying it on the ward). If learners return to an environment where experimentation is punished, the most technically excellent teaching session in the world will stop at Level 2. This session is not a warmup for Session 3 — it is Session 3's structural prerequisite.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Psychological safety named and discussed (Edmondson), meta-conversation about conditions for learning.

---

### 77–85 min | Commitment

Ask each participant to write down:
> *"One active learning moment you will design and deliver before Session 3. Specific: for which learners, when, using which approach. Post to the Padlet."*

Give three minutes to write and post.

> **Facilitator note:** Same principle as Session 1 — implementation intention requires specificity (Gollwitzer). *"I will try something different"* is not a commitment. *"On Thursday ward round, I will use a 2-minute think-pair-share after presenting the first patient instead of telling them the answer"* is.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Implementation intention, accountability, continuity across sessions.

---

### 85–90 min | Cliffhanger

End on this, exactly:

> *"You have now designed teaching that is active, grounded in evidence, and adapted to the reality of your ward. You have thought carefully about what your learners need and how to give them agency.*
>
> *Here is what I want you to sit with until next time:*
>
> *You check back in six weeks. You look your learners in the eye and you ask: 'What changed in your practice?' And they say... nothing.*
>
> *What do you do with that?"*

No resolution. Close the session.

> **Facilitator note:** This is not a pessimistic ending — it is a realistic one that creates genuine cognitive drive toward Session 3. The answer to *"what do you do with 'nothing'?"* is the entire content of Session 3. Do not give them the answer now.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Productive dissonance, forward-pacing, intrinsic motivation.
