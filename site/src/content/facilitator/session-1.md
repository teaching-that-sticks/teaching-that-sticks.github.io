## Learning outcomes

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Explain the principles of cognitive load theory and dual coding as applied to slide design
2. Identify accessibility problems in commonly used clinical teaching materials
3. Redesign at least one slide using evidence-based principles
4. Articulate the difference between slides that serve the learner and slides that serve the presenter's anxiety

---

| Theorist | Key idea in this session |

| Knowles (1984) | Andragogy — adult learners need relevance, self-direction, and connection to experience |

---

### Room setup

Arrive 20 minutes early. Arrange seating in a horseshoe or cabaret groups. Never classroom rows. This is not accidental and will be noticed. If asked, say nothing — add it to the debrief at minute 57.

Nothing on screen when participants arrive. Projector off, or showing a plain dark/blank slide. This is deliberate. The slight discomfort it creates is the first data point of the session.

Flipchart at the front with one question already written, turned away from the room:

> **What makes a great teacher?**

### Tech prep

- Word cloud tool ready to launch, question loaded: *"What do you remember from the slide you just saw?"* — do not reveal this yet
- Padlet set up, QR code printed or ready to share on screen, titled: *Teaching That Sticks — Session 1 Commitments*
- Your slide deck uses ONLY images and spoken word. Zero bullet points, zero dense text. You are the exemplar. If you violate this, you have undermined your own session before it has started.

### Preparing the "bad slide"

Create one genuinely catastrophic slide. Include all of the following:

- 11 or more bullet points
- At least two font sizes, one below 14pt
- Clip art or a low-resolution stock image, unrelated to the content
- WordArt or decorative text treatment
- A colour scheme with insufficient contrast
- A graph with no axis labels or legend
- Title: something recognisably clinical — *"Patient Safety Update — All Staff"* or similar

**This is not a parody.** Make it look exactly like a real slide that exists somewhere in your organisation — because it does. The more recognisable it is to participants, the harder it lands.

## Session flow

---

### 0–5 min | Arrival

Participants arrive to: no screen, unusual seating, flipchart turned away. Facilitator is in the room welcoming people, not standing at the front.

When everyone is seated, turn the flipchart to reveal the question. Say nothing else. Let them read it.

> **Facilitator note:** Resist the urge to introduce yourself at length or explain what is about to happen. The question and the silence are doing pedagogical work. Let it sit for 10–15 seconds before giving the instruction.

---

### 5–12 min | Think-pair-share: what makes a great teacher?

**Instruction:**
> *"Take 90 seconds on your own. Write down three things that make a great teacher. Then share with the person next to you."*

After pairs have spoken (approximately three minutes), take a whole-group share. Invite four or five responses. Write the keywords on the flipchart. Acknowledge each briefly — "yes", "good", "what else?" — without elaborating.

> **Facilitator note:** Nobody ever writes "good slides." Note this. You will return to it at minute 57. You are also demonstrating: think-pair-share (Vygotsky, ZPD), that this programme takes their existing knowledge seriously, and that learning happens through dialogue before it happens through transmission.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Activating prior knowledge, think-pair-share, adult learning principle of valuing experience.

---

### 12–22 min | The bad slide — live cognitive load demonstration

Say:
> *"I am going to show you a slide. Look at it carefully."*

Project the bad slide. Set a visible timer for **30 seconds exactly**. Then switch the projector off, or move to a blank slide. No warning that it is about to disappear.

Launch the word cloud immediately:
> *"Without looking at your notes — what do you remember?"*

Give participants 60–90 seconds to enter responses. Display the word cloud live.

The result will be sparse and fragmented, dominated by whatever was in the largest font or most visually dominant position. Sit with it for a moment.

> **Facilitator note:** Do not explain what happened. Ask: *"Interesting. Why do you think that is?"* Take two or three responses. Write *"Cognitive load?"* on the flipchart. Say: *"We'll come back to that in a few minutes."* The theory follows the experience — not the other way round. This is Kolb's cycle enacted.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Experience before theory (Kolb), Sweller's cognitive load demonstrated before it is named.

---

### 22–37 min | Didactic input: dual coding, cognitive load, accessibility

**15 minutes maximum. Set a visible timer.**

Deliver this section using only images and spoken word. No bullet points. No text-heavy slides. If you find yourself putting words on a slide here, stop — you are undoing the lesson in real time.

#### 1. Cognitive load — Sweller (1988) — 4 minutes

- Working memory holds approximately four items simultaneously (Miller 1956, revised by Cowan 2001)
- Three types of load: *intrinsic* (complexity of the content itself), *extraneous* (how it is presented), *germane* (effort invested in learning)
- Only extraneous load is within the designer's control
- That slide participants just saw? Extraneous load in every element
- Suggested image: a crowded noisy market versus a clean well-lit shop. Same products. Radically different cognitive experience.

#### 2. Dual coding — Mayer (2001) — 4 minutes

- Two processing channels: visual-spatial and auditory-verbal
- Text on a slide + spoken explanation = both channels processing the same information simultaneously = redundancy effect = less learning
- Image on a slide + spoken explanation = two channels processing different, complementary information = more learning
- Suggested image: a simple diagram of the dual-channel model (deliberately self-referential)

#### 3. Accessibility — 4 minutes

- Contrast ratios matter: WCAG 2.1 AA standard requires 4.5:1 for normal text
- Minimum 24pt for anything participants need to read from a projector
- Never use colour as the *only* means of conveying information
- Alt text matters for digital slides that will be shared
- Core principle: *accessible design is good design* — if it passes accessibility checks, it is almost always cleaner for everyone
- Suggested image: same information, inaccessible version beside accessible version — the improvement is immediately visible

#### 4. Whose anxiety does this slide serve? — Biggs (1996) — 3 minutes

- Constructive alignment: every element of a teaching design should exist to serve a learning outcome
- The 47-bullet-point slide is a security blanket, not a teaching tool
- Ask: *"Has anyone ever put everything they needed to say on a slide so they would not forget it?"* Hands will go up. *"That is teaching for yourself, not for your learner."*

> **Facilitator note:** When the timer goes off, stop. Even mid-sentence if necessary. Say: *"And that is 15 minutes of didactic. Notice how that felt."* If you overrun here you have undermined your own lesson. The timer is visible and deliberate — it models the principle.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Multimedia principle (you are the exemplar), time-bounded didactic, showing the pedagogy.

---

### 37–57 min | Slide redesign activity

**Instructions:**
> *"You are going to redesign a slide. You can use the bad slide I showed you, or — better — open the worst slide you currently use in your own teaching. You have 15 minutes in pairs or threes. The brief: apply what you have just heard. The constraints: no more than one key idea per slide, image and spoken word only, must pass a basic accessibility check."*

Circulate. Ask questions rather than give answers:

- *"What is the single thing you want someone to walk away knowing from this slide?"*
- *"If I turned the projector off after 30 seconds, what would they remember?"*
- *"Who is this slide for — you, or them?"*

#### Presenting back

Each pair or three takes two minutes. Use one of these formats:

**Gallery walk** — post slides on the wall or display on screen in sequence, participants rotate and add a sticky note: one thing that works, one thing they would change.

**Live crit** — each pair presents, group gives structured feedback using this frame:

| Prompt | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| What works? | Identifies principles in action, not just preference |
| What would you change? | Specific, evidence-referenced |
| Why does it matter for the learner? | Keeps feedback anchored to pedagogy, not aesthetics |

> **Facilitator note:** The feedback frame matters. Without it, feedback defaults to personal preference. The third question — *why does it matter for the learner?* — is the one that develops pedagogical reasoning rather than design taste.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Active learning, peer teaching, constructivism, assessment as learning.

---

### 57–67 min | Debrief: what shifted?

Whole group. Work through these questions — do not rush, do not fill the silence:

1. *"Look back at the flipchart — your answers to 'what makes a great teacher?' Did anyone say 'good slides'?"* (They did not.) *"So why do we spend so much time and anxiety on them?"*

2. *"Biggs says every element of a teaching design should exist to serve a learning outcome. Does your current slide deck pass that test?"*

3. *"What is one thing that shifted for you in the last hour?"*

Write responses on the flipchart. This is their debrief, not yours. Facilitate; do not lecture.

> **Facilitator note:** Watch for the participant who says *"but my organisation requires a certain slide template."* This is real and valid. Acknowledge it directly: *"That constraint is real. What is within your control inside that constraint?"* Do not dismiss it or promise it will be solved.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Metacognition, constructive alignment (Biggs), making the hidden curriculum visible.

---

### 67–77 min | Andragogy: know your learner

Ask:
> *"Your learners are experienced adults who have seen hundreds of slides. They have prior clinical knowledge, strong opinions, and very limited time. What do they actually need from you?"*

Brief input — five minutes maximum:

**Knowles' four principles of andragogy:**

1. **Self-direction** — give learners agency in their own learning
2. **Prior experience** — treat it as a resource, not a barrier or a blank slate
3. **Problem-centred** — connect to real clinical problems, not abstract theory
4. **Immediate relevance** — *"what can I use on Monday?"*

Link explicitly to the session: *"This is why we started with your experience, not a lecture. This is why the redesign activity used your own slides. The expertise was already in the room. Your job as a teacher is to activate it, not replace it."*

> **Facilitator note:** This is the moment to briefly bridge to Session 2 — *"Next time, we go further. What if no slide at all is the better answer?"* — but do not dwell on it. The cliffhanger comes at minute 88.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Andragogy enacted and named, constructivism.

---

### 77–85 min | Commitment

Ask each participant to identify:
> *"One slide you will redesign before Session 2. Specific — which slide, in which presentation, for which learners. Posted to the Padlet by [name the date — ideally 48 hours before Session 2]."*

Share the Padlet QR code. Give three minutes to write and post.

> **Facilitator note:** Push for specificity. *"I will look at my slides"* is not a commitment — it is an intention, and intentions alone predict almost nothing (Gollwitzer, 1999). A commitment with a named slide, a named session, and a named date is an implementation intention. That is what predicts behaviour change.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Implementation intention (Gollwitzer), accountability, spaced retrieval hook for Session 2.

---

### 85–90 min | Cliffhanger

End on this, exactly as written:

> *"You have made better slides. You know why they matter and how to build them. But here is the question I want you to sit with until next time:*
>
> *What if the best slide you could show your learners... is no slide at all?"*

No explanation. No softening. Close the session.

> **Facilitator note:** Do not add *"we'll explore that next time."* Leave it open. The unresolved question is what creates intrinsic motivation to return. Cognitive dissonance is the pedagogical mechanism. Trust it.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Productive cognitive dissonance, intrinsic motivation, spaced practice hook.

- Review the Padlet before Session 2 — you will open by pulling directly from it
