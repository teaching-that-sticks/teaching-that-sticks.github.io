## Learning outcomes

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Explain the Kirkpatrick model and identify where current clinical teaching sits within it
2. Design a 30/60/90 day follow-up plan that supports transfer of learning to practice
3. Facilitate a basic action learning set and a debrief using the good judgement approach
4. Apply advocacy-inquiry when a learner reports that nothing has changed

### Room setup

Arrange seating in triads from the start — this signals that the action learning set activity (minutes 43–58) is the structural centrepiece of this session.

Padlet from Sessions 1 and 2 visible on screen as participants arrive. They should be able to see the full arc of their commitments and contributions.

### Retrieval quiz

Prepare five questions drawn from Sessions 1 and 2. Suggested questions (adapt to what actually happened in your sessions):

1. Sweller identified three types of cognitive load. Name them. Which one is in the teacher's control?
2. What is the difference between assessment *of*, *for*, and *as* learning?
3. Mayer's multimedia principle says image + spoken word outperforms text + spoken word. Why?
4. Edmondson (1999) defines psychological safety as what?
5. You want your learner to remember something in six weeks. According to Ebbinghaus, what is the most important variable?

Print these on cards (one per participant) or display on screen. Low stakes, no grades, no judgment.

## Session flow

---

### 0–8 min | Retrieval quiz: sessions 1 and 2

Distribute quiz cards or display questions on screen. No introduction — just:
> *"Five questions. On your own. No looking back at notes. You have four minutes."*

After four minutes, pairs compare answers. Then whole-group check — facilitator gives the answers briefly, invites additions or disagreements.

> **Facilitator note:** The purpose here is not assessment — it is retrieval practice, which strengthens memory traces (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006). Name this explicitly after checking answers: *"What you just did is more powerful for long-term retention than re-reading your notes or attending this session again. Every time you retrieve, you consolidate. That is the science. We have been doing it across all three sessions — did you notice?"*

**Pedagogy modelled:** Spaced retrieval AND formative assessment — experienced AND named, making the hidden curriculum visible.

---

### 8–18 min | Word cloud: what changed?

Launch the word cloud:
> *"Since Session 1, what changed in your practice? It doesn't have to be big. What did you actually do differently?"*

Display responses live. Receive them with curiosity, not judgment. Some will be rich. Some will say: nothing. Both are data.

For responses that indicate no change, use advocacy-inquiry (Argyris & Schön):
> *"Tell me more about that — what got in the way?"*

Do not move past this quickly. Common answers: time, busyness, feeling unsure, resistance from colleagues, a system that does not reward it. Write them on the flipchart. All are real.

> **Facilitator note:** This is one of the hardest moments to facilitate well. Resist the temptation to reassure, to solve, or to explain away the *"nothing changed"* responses. They are honest and important. The advocacy-inquiry model asks you to assume positive intent and get curious before problem-solving. Say: *"I am not surprised, and I am not disappointed. What I am is curious about what would need to be different."* That question is the intervention.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Advocacy-inquiry modelled live (Argyris & Schön, Rudolph et al), psychological safety, receiving difficulty without flinching.

---

### 18–28 min | Kirkpatrick and the ward as learning environment

Brief input — 10 minutes:

**The Kirkpatrick model:**

| Level | Question | What you are measuring |
|-------|----------|------------------------|
| 1 — Reaction | Did they like it? | Satisfaction, perceived relevance |
| 2 — Learning | Did they learn it? | Knowledge, skill, attitude change |
| 3 — Behaviour | Did they use it? | Change in practice on the ward |
| 4 — Results | Did it make a difference? | Patient outcomes, team performance, culture |

Most clinical teaching is evaluated only at Level 1 — a post-session happy sheet. Some reaches Level 2. Level 3 is rare. Level 4 is rarer still.

*"We are at Levels 1 and 2 right now. How do we get to Level 3 — and what does that actually look like?"*

**The ward as learning environment:**

Reference Lave & Wenger (1991): the ward is not where you apply classroom learning. It *is* the learning environment. The 10-minute huddle teach, the post-ward-round debrief, the *"why did we do it this way?"* question at the bedside — these are not supplementary. They are where the real learning happens.

Legitimate peripheral participation: less experienced practitioners learn by being part of the community of practice, not by sitting in a classroom. Your job is to make that participation visible, deliberate, and safe.

> **Facilitator note:** This reframe — from classroom-as-primary to ward-as-primary — is a threshold concept for many clinical educators (Meyer & Land, 2003). It can feel destabilising: *"Does that mean what I do in the classroom doesn't matter?"* Name this directly: *"It means the classroom is the launch pad. The ward is where landing happens. Both matter."*

**Pedagogy modelled:** Kirkpatrick introduced and immediately applied, situated cognition (Lave & Wenger), threshold concept named.

---

### 28–43 min | Didactic input: the sticking toolkit

**15 minutes maximum. Timer visible.**

#### 1. Spaced repetition plan — 4 minutes

Forgetting is not a learner failure. It is the default, and it is designed into how most teaching currently works. A spaced repetition plan is not complicated: it is a decision, in advance, about when you will revisit material.

Minimum viable plan:
- One retrieval prompt at 48 hours (a single question by email or Padlet)
- One brief practice at one week (a 5-minute quiz at the start of the next ward round)
- A structured check-in at 30 days

The decision to space is more important than the method used.

#### 2. The 10-minute huddle teach — 4 minutes

Structure for a ward-based micro-teach:

| Element | Time | Purpose |
|---------|------|---------|
| Hook | 1 min | One question or problem — activate prior knowledge |
| Core | 5 min | One concept, one example, one clinical application |
| Check | 2 min | Ask: *"What would you do if..."* — retrieval, not recitation |
| Commit | 2 min | One person names one thing they will try before the next shift |

This is complete learning design in 10 minutes. It passes the constructive alignment test: outcome → activity → check.

#### 3. Debrief with good judgement — Rudolph et al (2006) — 4 minutes

Two contrasting approaches:
- **Debrief with good judgement:** the facilitator holds a private assumption that the learner had good reasons for what they did — then asks, with genuine curiosity, to understand those reasons before problem-solving
- **Debrief with a hidden agenda:** the facilitator has already decided what was wrong and uses questions to lead the learner there

The core move: **advocacy-inquiry**
> *"I noticed [specific behaviour]. I was thinking [my interpretation]. Help me understand your thinking."*

This is not neutral — it is a power-sharing move. It signals that the learner's mental model matters.

#### 4. Feedback and feedforward — Hattie & Timperley (2007) — 3 minutes

Three questions that structure all useful feedback:
1. **Where am I going?** (What is the goal?)
2. **How am I going?** (What progress is being made?)
3. **Where to next?** (What needs to change?)

Most clinical feedback answers only question 2, briefly, and then stops. Feedforward — question 3 — is the most powerful and the most neglected.

> **Facilitator note:** If running short on time, compress or cut the feedforward section — it is covered more fully in the action learning set that follows.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Content delivered by the means it describes (time-boxed, structured, immediately applicable).

---

### 43–58 min | Action learning set simulation

**Setup:** Triads (the seating arrangement you have already created). Assign roles:

- **Issue-holder:** brings a real teaching challenge they are currently facing
- **Questioners (x2):** ask questions only — no advice, no solutions, no *"have you tried..."* for the first eight minutes

**Round 1 — listening and questioning (8 minutes):**
> *"Issue-holder: describe your challenge in two minutes. Then the questioners have six minutes to ask questions only. No advice. No 'I had the same thing.' Questions only. Aim for advocacy-inquiry: 'Tell me more about...', 'What have you already tried?', 'What would a good outcome look like?'"*

**Round 2 — collaborative problem-solving (5 minutes):**
> *"Now the questioners can offer ideas and observations. Issue-holder: you do not have to take any of them. You are in charge of your own problem."*

**Brief whole-group reflection (2 minutes):**
> *"What was hardest about that? What did you notice?"*

> **Facilitator note:** The hardest thing in an ALS is not giving advice. Clinicians are trained to solve problems rapidly — withholding that impulse for eight minutes is genuinely uncomfortable. That discomfort is productive: it is the thing Revans (1982) identified as the condition for action learning to work. Name it: *"The discomfort you felt is evidence of learning. You were outside your habitual response pattern. That is exactly where change happens."*

**Pedagogy modelled:** Action learning set structure (Revans), advocacy-inquiry enacted, peer learning, psychological safety tested safely.

---

### 58–68 min | The sticking plan

Individual work, then pair share.

**Worksheet prompt** (distribute or display):

> *Your sticking plan*
>
> **The learner group I am thinking about:**
>
> **What I taught them / will teach them:**
>
> **30 days:** What will I do to follow up? Who is responsible? What counts as success?
>
> **60 days:** Same questions.
>
> **90 days:** Same questions — and if nothing has changed, what will my response be?
>
> **The minimum viable ward intervention if time is scarce:**
>
> **Who will hold me accountable for this plan?**

Give eight minutes to complete individually, then two minutes to share with a partner.

> **Facilitator note:** The *"what if nothing changed?"* question at 90 days is the most important one on the sheet. Most follow-up plans stop when the answer is positive. The plan that prepares for the *"no"* answer — and responds with advocacy-inquiry rather than disappointment — is the one that actually creates change over time.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Kirkpatrick Level 3 planning made concrete, implementation intention, planning for failure.

---

### 68–76 min | The whole person: hierarchy of needs

This conversation must be handled carefully. It is not optional.

Say:
> *"Before we close, I want to name something that has probably been in the room across all three sessions.*
>
> *Sometimes the barrier to learning is not pedagogical. Sometimes your learner did not complete the task because they are working two jobs to pay their rent. Sometimes the debrief feels pointless because they are being bullied by the registrar. Sometimes the forgetting curve is not the problem — exhaustion, grief, financial stress, or a home situation that is not safe is the problem.*
>
> *Maslow (1943) described a hierarchy of needs: the physiological and safety needs must be substantially met before the higher-order needs — belonging, esteem, self-actualisation — become motivators. Underperformance at work is usually a problem elsewhere first.*
>
> *Debrief with good judgement is not just a pedagogical tool. It is a welfare tool. When you sit with a learner and ask with genuine curiosity 'help me understand what got in the way,' you might get a pedagogical answer. You might also get something much more important.*
>
> *You do not need to solve it. You need to see it, name it, and connect the person to the right support. That is also teaching.*"

Invite brief responses. Do not force dialogue — this can land differently for different people.

> **Facilitator note:** This is where compassionate clinical leadership and clinical education converge. Some participants will find this obvious; some will find it confronting; some will have a personal response to it. Hold the space. You do not need to wrap this up neatly. It is supposed to feel weighty.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Whole-person approach, Maslow named and applied, debrief with good judgement as welfare practice.

---

### 76–83 min | Commitment cards

Distribute the commitment cards (printed). Participants write, individually and in silence:

1. **One thing I will do differently** — specific, observable, timed
2. **The person who will hold me accountable** — named, informed, willing
3. **The date I will check back** — in their calendar, now

They keep the card. It is not collected.

> **Facilitator note:** The card is theirs, not yours. This is intentional. You are modelling self-direction (Knowles). If you collect it, you become the accountability mechanism — and the programme cannot follow every participant for 90 days. The named peer is the accountability mechanism. That is sustainable and respectful.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Implementation intention, self-direction, peer accountability.

---

### 83–88 min | Meta-reflection: what did you notice?

Ask the group:
> *"Look back across all three sessions. What pedagogical principles did you spot in how this programme was designed and run?"*

Wait. Let them find them. They will. Biggs, Ebbinghaus, Knowles, Kolb, Edmondson — let participants name these back. Add anything significant they miss, briefly.

Then:
> *"This is the whole game. You just experienced what it feels like to be taught by someone who designed for your learning, not for their own comfort. That is what your learners need from you."*

> **Facilitator note:** This is the highest-order transfer moment in the programme. When a participant says *"you used spaced retrieval at the start of every session"* or *"the room layout was always different — that was deliberate, wasn't it?"* — that is the threshold concept crossing (Meyer & Land). They are no longer thinking about teaching as content delivery. They are thinking like learning architects.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Making the hidden curriculum visible, meta-cognition, threshold concept — learning architect identity.

---

### 88–90 min | Final challenge — not a close

End on this. Not a summary. Not a thank you. This:

> *"You now know what amazing looks like.*
>
> *You have seen it. You have been in it. You have the evidence, the theory, the tools, and the plan.*
>
> *One question:*
>
> *What will YOU do about it?"*

Leave the room.

> **Facilitator note:** No applause prompt, no *"any final questions,"* no closing slide. The open question is the final pedagogical move. It refuses to resolve the productive dissonance. Learning continues after the room. Let it.

**Pedagogy modelled:** Productive dissonance, identity challenge, learning as ongoing — not a closed event.

- Set a calendar reminder for 30, 60, and 90-day Level 3 follow-up surveys
