## The starting position

NHS operational leaders are not opposed to good education. Most of them know, intuitively, that the current defaults don't work as well as they should. What they are contending with is a set of pressures — budgets constrained, diaries overcommitted, boards requiring demonstrable delivery — that push developmental activity to the back of the queue. Good ideas that don't immediately translate into a visible result on a dashboard tend to stay theoretical.

The challenge for anyone trying to introduce this programme is not usually persuasion. It is timing, framing, and fit. The goal is to connect what this programme offers to things that operational leaders are already accountable for — so that running it looks like a response to existing pressure, not an addition to it.

This page is a practical framework for doing that.

---

## The core framing

The most effective opening is not a pedagogical argument. It is a question about outcomes that the other person is already responsible for:

> "Are we getting the result we actually want from the way we currently do this?"

This matters because senior leaders already understand that some educational approaches work better than others — the issue is usually that improving education hasn't been visibly connected to the outcomes they're being held to account for. The framing that works is not "we should do education differently"; it is "here is a way to get more return on the time we're already spending".

## A pragmatic sequence

### 1. Find the pain point they already own

Avoid starting with "we should invest in educator development." Start instead with a problem that is already irritating the people who control diaries and budgets:

- Mandatory training that has to be repeated because the first run didn't stick
- Competency concerns that don't resolve despite multiple sessions on the same material
- New starters who pass induction but can't transfer skills to unsupervised practice
- Engagement and morale problems in education settings
- Complaints about training quality from staff or learners

Once you have identified one, the question becomes: "Is the current educational approach likely to fix this?" Usually, honestly examined, the answer is no.

### 2. Translate into their language

Pedagogical language can accidentally signal abstraction or academic distance. Translate before you speak:

| Instead of… | Say… |
|-------------|------|
| "The session lacks active learning" | "The current format is unlikely to produce reliable practice change" |
| "We need spaced repetition" | "A one-off session won't build the kind of memory that survives the next busy shift" |
| "Poor cognitive load management" | "The slides are trying to do too much — people can't process and remember at that density" |
| "We need formative assessment" | "There's no way of knowing who actually understood and who didn't" |

The underlying argument is the same. The framing determines whether it lands.

### 3. Frame it as systemic, not personal

Most clinical educators were never formally developed as educators — they stepped into the role from clinical seniority, inherited approaches that were operationally convenient, and have been doing their best with what they were given. That is not a criticism; it is the structural reality of how healthcare education works.

A framing that lands well:
> "Most of us were never actually taught how to teach — we picked up what was around us. This programme is about giving people what the system never got round to providing."

That positions the programme as addressing a gap in provision, not correcting a personal failure. It is also accurate.

### 4. Find your early adopters

Every organisation has people who are already asking these questions — clinicians who find slide-heavy teaching frustrating, practice educators who have sensed for years that something isn't working, ward leaders who are puzzled by why the same competency gaps keep recurring. These are your natural first cohort.

Starting with people who are already curious means the pilot runs with energy rather than resistance, the evaluation data is more likely to be positive, and those participants become credible internal advocates for the next cohort. You don't need a formal mandate to have a first conversation with people who are already interested.

### 5. Pilot one thing — and make it visible

Don't propose an education strategy. Propose redesigning one session, one induction component, or one supervision process. Something small enough to be tried without significant political capital, specific enough to be measured.

Run it differently. Then collect evidence:
- Did engagement look different?
- What did the L1 evaluations say?
- Could participants recall the content two weeks later?
- Did the behaviour you were trying to change actually change?

The logic here is simple: a concrete result is more persuasive than a theoretical argument, particularly in environments where diaries are tight and every commitment competes with clinical service demand. One visible success — better engagement, stronger recall, reduced repeat queries — gives the programme a footprint in the organisation's own evidence base, not just in the literature.

## After the first run

Once there is one credible result in front of people — evaluations, observable change in how participants design their sessions, a reduction in the problem that prompted the conversation in the first place — the broader conversation becomes easier:

- Educator development as a planned activity rather than an ad hoc event
- Protected time for CPD that is genuinely developmental rather than mandatory-training compliance
- Kirkpatrick-level evaluation as a normal expectation, not an unusual request
- The programme running again, for the next cohort, without having to make the case from scratch

That is a realistic three-session outcome. It doesn't require a strategy paper — it requires a first cohort and the evidence they generate.

---

## A realistic scope

This programme is not a structural fix. It will not resolve workforce shortages, create protected time that doesn't currently exist, or substitute for a fully-resourced education team. NHS organisations are contending with pressures that no CPD programme can undo.

What it can do, in a focused and low-cost way, is shift what happens in the room when clinical educators teach. That is a narrow target — and deliberately so. A programme that tries to fix everything fixes nothing. One that reliably changes the default teaching approach of the people who go through it has a traceable effect on every learner those educators subsequently work with.

The case for the investment is proportionate to the cost: this programme is free. The ask is protected time. For that, the threshold for a positive return is correspondingly low.
