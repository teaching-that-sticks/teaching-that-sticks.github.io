## Teaching That Sticks — the theoretical foundations

*Read this once before your first delivery. It covers why each framework is in this programme and gives you language for the moments when someone challenges it. Full academic context and paper references for each theorist are in the [Theory Library](/theories).*

*Designed to be read in 15 minutes.*

---

## John Biggs — Constructive Alignment

[Full theory →](/theories/constructive-alignment)

**Why it's in this programme:** It is the spine. Biggs gives you the diagnostic question for everything: *"Does this element of the teaching design actually cause the learning you intend?"* The 47-bullet-point slide fails this test not because it is ugly, but because it does not align with any plausible learning outcome.

**If someone pushes back:** *"But we don't always know our outcomes in advance — clinical teaching is too dynamic."* Response: *"Fair point — Biggs is describing formal teaching design. In the moment, the principle still applies: what do I actually want this learner to be able to do after this encounter? If you can't answer that, you're designing for yourself, not for them."*

---

## John Sweller — Cognitive Load Theory

[Full theory →](/theories/cognitive-load)

**Why it's in this programme:** Every element of a cluttered slide increases extraneous load. Understanding this shifts the conversation from aesthetics to function: extraneous load is measurably preventing learning.

**If someone pushes back:** *"My learners like having all the information on the slide so they can look back at it later."* Response: *"That's a document, not a slide. Documents and slides serve different purposes. If learners need reference material, give them a handout."*

---

## Richard Mayer — Multimedia Learning

[Full theory →](/theories/dual-coding)

**Why it's in this programme:** It gives the evidence base for the specific design change being asked of participants — replace text with images. Mayer has run over 100 experiments producing consistent results.

**If someone pushes back:** *"This was tested in lab conditions with undergraduate students — it won't translate to clinical ward teaching."* Response: *"Fair methodological point. But the mechanism — that working memory channels can be used more or less efficiently — is not context-specific."*

---

## Lev Vygotsky — Zone of Proximal Development

[Full theory →](/theories/zone-of-proximal-development)

**Why it's in this programme:** Think-pair-share is the practical application of ZPD in Session 1. By asking participants to discuss with a peer before engaging with theory, you activate what they already know and use the pair conversation to extend it.

**If someone pushes back:** *"This is a primary school concept — surely it doesn't apply to experienced adult professionals."* Response: *"The ZPD applies anywhere there's a gap between current and potential capability — which is everywhere, at every career stage."*

---

## Malcolm Knowles — Andragogy

[Full theory →](/theories/andragogy)

**Why it's in this programme:** The reason Session 1 starts with their experience (think-pair-share, their own worst slides) rather than a lecture is because Knowles tells us this is the correct sequence for adult learners. Every facilitator note that says *"the expertise is already in the room"* is Knowles in action.

**If someone pushes back:** *"My learners are junior — surely they need more direction?"* Response: *"Knowles is describing a spectrum. More junior practitioners need more structure. The principle is: always check what experience they bring and build from it."*

---

## Hermann Ebbinghaus — The Forgetting Curve

[Full theory →](/theories/spaced-practice)

**Why it's in this programme:** Ebbinghaus is the problem diagnosis for Session 3's entire argument. The reason clinical learning often produces no behaviour change is not learner motivation — it is that forgetting is the default state and follow-up was not designed.

**If someone pushes back:** *"My learners retain much more than that — they can recall content from years ago."* Response: *"You're right that procedural memory degrades more slowly than declarative memory. The curve is most acute for declarative knowledge — exactly the kind most study days deliver."*

---

## David Kolb — Experiential Learning Cycle

[Full theory →](/theories/experiential-learning)

**Why it's in this programme:** The session structure is Kolb's cycle in explicit form. The bad slide demonstration is stage 1. *"Why do you think you remembered those things?"* is stage 2. The 15-minute didactic is stage 3. The redesign activity is stage 4.

**Note:** Do not invoke Kolb's Learning Styles Inventory — it is poorly evidenced and largely discredited. The cycle is useful; the styles typology is not.

---

## Jean Lave & Etienne Wenger — Situated Learning

[Full theory →](/theories/situated-learning)

**Why it's in this programme:** The ward is not where you apply classroom learning — it is where the real learning happens. The 10-minute huddle teach is *legitimate peripheral participation* — less experienced practitioners learning by being part of the community.

**If someone pushes back:** *"But patients can't be used as learning opportunities — patient safety comes first."* Response: *"The teaching happens *around* clinical care, not *at the expense of* it."*

---

## Amy Edmondson — Psychological Safety

[Full theory →](/theories/psychological-safety)

**Why it's in this programme:** It is the non-negotiable precondition for everything else. Think-pair-share requires the willingness to share with a colleague. Active learning requires the willingness to get things wrong in public.

**If someone pushes back:** *"Psychological safety is just making excuses for poor performance."* Response: *"Psychological safety and accountability are orthogonal, not opposites. High safety + high accountability = the learning zone. You need both."*

---

## John Hattie & Helen Timperley — Feedback and Feedforward

[Full theory →](/theories/feedback-feedforward)

**Why it's in this programme:** The debrief with good judgement structure in Session 3 is built on these three questions. The commitment card's specificity requirement is feedforward.

**If someone pushes back:** *"We don't have time for elaborate feedback structures in a busy clinical environment."* Response: *"The three questions take about 90 seconds to answer together, verbally, at the bedside."*

---

## Reg Revans — Action Learning

[Full theory →](/theories/action-learning)

**Why it's in this programme:** The ALS simulation in Session 3 is the embodied version of everything the programme has been building toward: psychological safety, peer learning, questioning over advice, and learning from actual challenges of professional practice.

**If someone pushes back:** *"This is just a peer support group — how is it learning?"* Response: *"Revans distinguishes action learning from peer support by its focus on a specific problem and a commitment to act."*

---

## Daniel Kirkpatrick — Four-Level Evaluation

[Full theory →](/theories/kirkpatrick-model)

**Why it's in this programme:** Without evaluation there is no evidence this programme works. Kirkpatrick also gives participants a simple language for critiquing evaluation in their own contexts.

**If someone pushes back:** *"Kirkpatrick is outdated."* Response: *"True — the New World Kirkpatrick Model (2016) extends the original. But for a clinical audience needing a simple, credible framework they can apply immediately, the four-level structure is still the most accessible entry point."*

---

## Meyer & Land — Threshold Concepts

[Full theory →](/theories/threshold-concepts)

**Why it's in this programme:** The central threshold concept is the shift from *content deliverer* to *learning architect*. Naming it explicitly, including naming the discomfort, helps participants understand that discomfort as evidence of learning rather than inadequacy.

**If someone pushes back:** *"I'm not sure I've crossed this threshold."* Response: *"The fact that you're asking the question means you're in the liminal space — the uncomfortable middle. That is exactly where learning is happening."*

---

## Abraham Maslow — Hierarchy of Needs

[Full theory →](/theories/maslow)

**Why it's in this programme:** It provides the theoretical warrant for the most important clinical educator shift in Session 3: the move from *"this learner is underperforming"* to *"what else might be going on for this person?"*

**If someone pushes back:** *"This is too soft — in healthcare, people need to meet standards regardless of their personal circumstances."* Response: *"Standards must be maintained. Maslow is not an excuse for underperformance; it is an explanation that enables the right response."*

---

## Peter Gollwitzer — Implementation Intentions

[Full theory →](/theories/implementation-intentions)

**Why it's in this programme:** The commitment structures at the end of every session are implementation intentions. The difference between *"I'll try something different"* and *"On Thursday at the 9am ward round, I will ask one open question before giving the answer"* is the difference between an aspiration and a behaviour change.

---

## Rudolph, Simon, Dufresne & Raemer — Debrief with Good Judgement

[Full theory →](/theories/debriefing)

**Why it's in this programme:** It directly addresses the psychological safety problem in hierarchical clinical environments. The *debrief with bad judgement* approach — where the facilitator has already decided what was wrong — is common and produces defensiveness, not learning.

**If someone pushes back:** *"In an emergency, you can't use advocacy-inquiry — you have to give direct feedback."* Response: *"Correct — and the model is not for the resus room. It is for the structured learning conversation after the event."*
