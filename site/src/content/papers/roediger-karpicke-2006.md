## What the study did

Roediger and Karpicke ran two carefully controlled experiments investigating what students should do after an initial study period to maximise long-term retention. The dominant student strategy — and the dominant assumption behind instructional design — was restudying: reviewing material repeatedly should strengthen memory. Their hypothesis was that actively retrieving material from memory (taking a test) would outperform passive re-exposure (re-reading) for long-term retention, even when initial performance looked the other way.

Both experiments used prose passages as study material — short factual texts that participants read and then either re-studied or attempted to recall from memory, across varying numbers of study and test trials.

## What it found

**The testing effect is real and large:** Students who spent time retrieving information from memory (rather than re-reading it) showed substantially better retention at delayed testing — particularly at one week. The retrieval condition produced worse immediate recall in some conditions, but the positions reversed completely at longer delays.

**More testing beats more studying for long-term retention:** Participants who studied once and were then tested multiple times retained more at one week than participants who studied the same material four times with no testing. This held even when the tests were unguided recall — no cues, no feedback, just the attempt to retrieve.

**The testing effect applies even when the test is failed:** Attempting to retrieve information, and failing, still produces a memory benefit at delayed testing compared to passively reading the same information. The retrieval *attempt* — not just the retrieval *success* — does productive cognitive work.

**Why re-studying misleads:** Re-reading produces a feeling of familiarity and fluency that learners (and instructors) mistake for learning. It is relatively easy — the material feels known. But fluency is not retention. The sense that you understand something after reading it twice is not a reliable indicator that you will remember it next week.

## The one finding worth quoting in a meeting

*"Testing enhances learning more than additional study does, and the benefits of testing are especially large when learners are tested after a delay."*

For clinical education: every quiz, every *"what do you remember from last time?"*, every *"without looking — what are the three types of cognitive load?"* is doing more for retention than giving participants time to re-read their notes. The test is not the measurement of learning — it is the mechanism of it.

## Honest limitations

- Both experiments used prose passage recall — a specific, moderately complex learning task. Generalisation to clinical skills acquisition, procedural learning, or attitude change is not directly supported by this study
- The retrieval practice benefit varies with material complexity, time delays, and the nature of the test. Multiple-choice testing and free-recall testing have different profiles; this study used free-recall, which produces larger benefits than format-matched re-study but may not represent the full range of retrieval contexts
- The study measures retention of propositional knowledge (facts, ideas). Transfer to novel problems — the ability to apply knowledge in new situations — shows a more mixed picture in the wider retrieval practice literature
- Feedback after testing substantially improves outcomes; the benefit of unguided retrieval is real but smaller than retrieval with corrective feedback
- Publication bias in the retrieval practice literature likely inflates effect size estimates in meta-analyses

## What it means for your practice

Three applications in increasing order of commitment:

1. **Open every session by asking what people remember from last time** — not as a courtesy review, but as spaced retrieval. The cognitive work of trying to remember, with partial success, is the learning. Session 3 opens with a five-question quiz for exactly this reason.
2. **Replace some re-reading or note-checking time with attempted recall.** Any time a learner reviews their notes before a session, they would get more return from closing the notes and trying to recall what they cover first.
3. **Build in low-stakes tests at 24–48 hours after a session.** A single question by email — *"Without looking, what are the three types of cognitive load?"* — does more for one-week retention than sending a recap slide deck.

## How it appears in Teaching That Sticks

Roediger and Karpicke underpin the programme's spaced retrieval architecture. Session 2 opens by pulling up the Padlet commitments from Session 1 — not as an accountability exercise, but as retrieval. Session 3 opens with a five-question quiz on Sessions 1 and 2. These are structural applications of the testing effect: the sessions are spaced weeks apart precisely to exploit the long-delay retrieval benefit shown in this paper. Participants experience the principle before it is named.
