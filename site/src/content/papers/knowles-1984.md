## What the study did

Knowles's core thesis was that the dominant model of education — pedagogy — had been developed for teaching children, and that applying it unchanged to adult learners produced structurally poor results. Adults were not blank slates. They brought accumulated experience, established professional identities, and fundamentally different motivational structures to learning. *Andragogy in Action* compiled Knowles's earlier theoretical work with practitioner-contributed case studies across corporate, healthcare, government, and continuing education settings. It is less a controlled empirical study than a theoretical framework illustrated through applied cases.

The enduring value is the conceptual model, not the case study data.

## What it found

**The core principles of andragogy:**

**1. Self-concept:** As people mature, their self-concept shifts from dependency to self-direction. Adults resist being treated as passive recipients of instruction. Teaching that places the experienced professional in a dependent, empty-vessel role creates resistance before the content has even been delivered.

**2. Experience:** Adult learners bring accumulated experience that is itself a learning resource — not background noise to be managed, but the richest material in the room. Instructional designs that bypass this resource implicitly communicate that the learner's prior knowledge is irrelevant. They are also wasting the most credible, contextually rich evidence available.

**3. Readiness to learn:** Adults become ready to learn things that are directly relevant to their current social roles and developmental tasks. Timing matters. A registrar preparing to supervise junior doctors for the first time is ready to learn about teaching in a way that the same person five years earlier was not. Abstract curriculum that cannot be anchored to a current problem will meet lower engagement regardless of its quality.

**4. Orientation to learning:** Adults are problem-centred, not subject-centred. The question "how do I run a better ward round debrief?" is a better entry point than "let's study debriefing theory." The subject is a vehicle; the problem is the destination.

**5. Motivation to learn** (later addition): Adult learners are more responsive to internal motivators — professional identity, curiosity, competence — than to external ones like compliance requirements or grades. This does not mean external motivators have no effect; it means that instruction relying solely on them produces compliance, not learning.

**6. Need to know** (later addition): Before engaging with what or how, adults need to understand why. *"We are covering this because [specific clinical problem]"* is not a courtesy — it is a pedagogic precondition for engagement. In mandatory training contexts, failing to address the need to know is the single most common cause of the disengagement facilitators then attribute to the learners.

## The one finding worth quoting in a meeting

*"The richest resource for learning in most situations is the learners themselves — not the teacher, not the curriculum, not the materials. A teacher's first task is to develop procedures for tapping into that resource."*

For clinical education: every ward teaching session already has subject matter experts in it. The learners have been treating patients for years. The question is not what to tell them but how to help them articulate, test, and build on what they already know.

## Honest limitations

- Andragogy as a separate theory of adult learning has been critiqued; some researchers argue that the underlying cognitive mechanisms of learning do not fundamentally differ between children and adults — what changes is context, motivation, and prior knowledge, not the mechanism
- The case studies in the book are practitioner-reported and vary widely in rigour — they illustrate the principles rather than providing independent empirical support
- The four-to-six principles can be treated as characteristics of all adults, which overgeneralises. Many adults are dependent learners in specific contexts; many children are highly self-directed. The model describes tendencies, not invariants
- The model offers limited guidance for situations where adult learners resist self-direction or where andragogic methods encounter institutional cultures that actively counteract them — which is most NHS continuing education contexts
- Hartree (1984), writing in the same year, critiqued andragogy as an ideal description of the self-actualising adult learner rather than a theory with broad empirical applicability

## What it means for your practice

Three applications that follow directly from the framework:

1. **Start with their experience.** Before any didactic input, find out what participants already know and have already tried. The think-pair-share at the start of Session 1 is not an ice-breaker — it is using the room as the primary curriculum resource.
2. **Answer the question "why are we doing this?" explicitly.** Do not assume it is obvious. In mandatory training especially, the need-to-know is the first point of resistance and the cheapest one to address.
3. **Connect to the Monday-morning problem.** Whatever you teach, the question *"what does this look like on Thursday's ward round?"* should be answerable before participants leave. If it is not, you have not completed the learning design.

## How it appears in Teaching That Sticks

Knowles's framework is the structural rationale of the programme. Think-pair-share is andragogy enacted. Bringing your own worst slide is prior experience as curriculum. Commitment to specific, personally chosen implementation intentions is self-direction in practice. The use of real teaching problems rather than textbook examples is problem-centredness. When the facilitator says *"the expertise was already in the room — your job is to activate it, not replace it,"* that is Knowles.
