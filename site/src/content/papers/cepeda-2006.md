## What the study did

Cepeda and colleagues conducted the largest systematic review of the spacing effect (also called the distributed practice effect) at the time of publication. They analysed 839 samples drawn from 317 studies, spanning more than a century of research on how the timing of study affects memory. The key question was not just *"does spacing help?"* — the research strongly suggested it did — but *"how much spacing is optimal, and does it depend on how long you want to remember?"*

## What it found

**The spacing effect is real, robust, and general:** Distributing practice across time consistently outperforms massed practice (studying everything at once) for long-term retention. This held across all ages, content domains, and retention intervals studied. It is among the most replicated effects in cognitive psychology.

**The optimal gap depends on the retention interval:** This is the key practical finding. The ideal spacing between study sessions is roughly 10–20% of the total retention interval. If you want to remember something for one week, spacing study sessions one to two days apart is optimal. If you want to remember something for a year, spacing them five to six weeks apart is more effective. This is not intuitive — most people either space too little (everything the night before) or too much (a course run once a year).

**The expanding retrieval schedule:** Early in learning, short gaps are optimal. As the memory consolidates, longer gaps become more effective. This is the theoretical basis for spaced repetition software (e.g., Anki) and for the programme's architecture of sessions separated by weeks rather than days.

**The size of the effect:** Across the 839 samples, the average advantage of distributed over massed practice was substantial — roughly equivalent to the difference between forgetting most content within a week (massed) versus retaining much of it over months (distributed).

## The one finding worth quoting in a meeting

*"The optimal gap between study sessions is approximately 10–20% of the total retention interval. For annual clinical competency that you want retained all year, the relevant gap between retrieval prompts is not days — it is weeks."*

For mandatory training: if content must be retained for 12 months, one-off annual training is the worst possible spacing strategy. Brief monthly retrieval prompts would dramatically outperform it on retention, cost a fraction of the time, and require no additional room booking.

## Honest limitations

- Almost all included studies used verbal recall tasks with relatively simple materials — clinical procedural skills and complex clinical reasoning may involve different consolidation mechanisms
- The studies were conducted under controlled conditions; real clinical environments introduce competing demands, fatigue, and distraction that affect spacing effects
- The "optimal gap" recommendation is derived from laboratory conditions and represents an approximation for complex real-world content
- The study does not directly address *interleaving* (mixing content types), which has its own literature

## What it means for your practice

Three practical changes with strong evidence backing:

1. **Replace annual training with brief monthly retrieval.** A five-minute retrieval quiz at the start of a team meeting, twelve times a year, will produce better retention than one ninety-minute training event annually.
2. **Time your follow-up for just before forgetting.** The pre-session emails in Teaching That Sticks are sent 48 hours before each session — this is timed to prompt retrieval at the point of near-forgetting from the previous session.
3. **Name the mechanism.** When you use spaced retrieval in clinical teaching, tell learners why. *"I'm asking you this because your brain started forgetting it 48 hours ago — this prompt shifts the forgetting curve."* Learners who understand the mechanism are more likely to replicate it independently.

## How it appears in Teaching That Sticks

The programme's session spacing (sessions approximately three to four weeks apart) is a direct application of the spacing effect at the recommended interval for monthly retention. The pre-session retrieval prompts (Padlet and email), the opening retrieval activities in Sessions 2 and 3, and the 30 and 90-day follow-up surveys are all spaced retrieval events timed against the expected forgetting curve. Cepeda et al. provides the quantitative backing for why this spacing specifically, rather than weekly or annual, is used.
