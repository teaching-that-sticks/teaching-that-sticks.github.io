## What the study did

Gollwitzer and Sheeran conducted a meta-analysis of 94 independent studies examining whether forming implementation intentions — specific *if-then* plans of the form *"When situation X arises, I will do Y"* — increases the likelihood of goal achievement compared to forming goal intentions alone (*"I intend to achieve Z"*). The studies covered a wide range of goal domains including health behaviour, academic performance, and workplace tasks.

## What it found

**Effect size d = 0.65** — a medium-to-large effect. Across 94 studies, people who formed specific implementation intentions were significantly more likely to achieve their goals than people who formed equivalent goal intentions without the if-then specificity.

**The effect generalised broadly:** Implementation intentions improved goal achievement across:
- Health behaviours (diet, exercise, medication adherence, screening attendance)
- Academic tasks (essay completion, exam preparation)
- Interpersonal goals (managing anger, overcoming social anxiety)
- Workplace performance goals

**The mechanism is automaticity:** Implementation intentions work by creating a mental link between the anticipated situation (the *if* — the cue) and the intended behaviour (the *then* — the response). When the cue situation occurs, the behaviour is initiated automatically, without requiring fresh deliberation or conscious effort. The decision is effectively pre-made.

**Goal intentions are not enough:** Goal intentions predict stated motivation. They predict much less well whether someone actually does the thing. The gap between intention and behaviour — the *intention-behaviour gap* — is reliably closed by adding implementation intention specificity to the goal.

## The one finding worth quoting in a meeting

*"Across 94 studies, forming specific if-then plans produced a medium-to-large improvement in goal achievement (d = 0.65) compared to stating intentions alone. The mechanism is pre-made decisions — when you specify the when, where, and how in advance, the behaviour becomes automatic when the cue occurs."*

For clinical education: every commitment activity that ends with *"I'll try to teach better"* is a goal intention and will produce approximately what goal intentions always produce — good intentions that fail at the first competing demand. *"When I start handover on Thursdays, I will ask one retrieval question before giving any information"* is an implementation intention. The difference in follow-through is substantial and quantified.

## Honest limitations

- Most studies were conducted in non-clinical settings; translation to busy ward environments requires the additional friction of environmental complexity and competing demands
- Effect sizes from laboratory and self-report studies may overestimate real-world effects
- Some goal domains show stronger effects than others — goals that are already strongly desired benefit most from implementation intentions; weakly desired goals benefit less
- The meta-analysis does not tell us how long implementation intentions sustain behaviour change without reinforcement — the 30 and 90-day follow-up in the programme addresses this gap pragmatically

## What it means for your practice

Three changes:

1. **Redesign your commitment activities.** Any training session that ends with *"reflect on what you've learned"* or *"think about what you'll take forward"* is not a commitment activity. A commitment activity specifies when, where, and exactly what.
2. **Push for specificity.** When a participant says *"I'll try to use more retrieval practice,"* ask: *"Which session? Which day? What question will you ask?"* The specificity is the mechanism — vagueness is not a minor inconvenience, it is the primary predictor of non-completion.
3. **Build in the cue.** The most reliable implementation intentions link the behaviour to something that already reliably happens: *"When I write the care plan at 8am"*, *"When the patient is moved to the bay."* The cue should be something that occurs whether or not they remember the intention.

## How it appears in Teaching That Sticks

The commitment card at the end of Session 3, and the commitment activities at the close of Sessions 1 and 2, are explicitly structured as implementation intentions. The facilitator script at each commitment activity includes a push-back prompt: *"'I'll try to...' is not specific enough. When exactly? Which patients? What will you say?"* The 30-day and 90-day follow-up surveys are timed to coincide with the point at which implementation intentions begin to require reinforcement — a retrieval prompt for the commitment itself.
