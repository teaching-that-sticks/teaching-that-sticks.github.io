## What the study did

Kolb synthesised three major traditions in learning theory — Dewey's pragmatism, Lewin's experiential field theory, and Piaget's cognitive-developmental model — into a single framework for understanding adult learning. His central claim was that learning is not transmission of information but transformation of experience: genuine learning requires a cycle of having experiences, reflecting on them, deriving abstract principles, and testing those principles in new situations.

The book also introduced the Learning Styles Inventory (LSI), which maps individual preferences onto quadrants of the cycle. This component has attracted severe empirical critique (see limitations), but it has not diminished the influence of the cycle model itself.

## What it found

**The four-stage experiential learning cycle:**

**1. Concrete Experience (CE):** The learner encounters a real, simulated, or recalled experience. This is the raw material. Without it, everything that follows is abstract. A clinical educator who has never experienced cognitive overload in an audience cannot meaningfully teach about it.

**2. Reflective Observation (RO):** The learner observes and reflects on the experience from multiple perspectives — what happened, why it happened, how different people responded. This is the stage most commonly skipped in clinical teaching, where busyness rewards action over reflection. Without reflection, experience does not produce learning — it just produces more experience.

**3. Abstract Conceptualisation (AC):** From reflection, the learner derives generalisable principles or theoretical models. This is where the experience becomes knowledge that can be applied beyond this specific instance. Theory earns its place here — not as a preamble to experience, but as its explanation.

**4. Active Experimentation (AE):** The learner applies the derived principle to a new situation — testing whether it holds, generating new concrete experience, and restarting the cycle. This stage distinguishes learning that changes practice from learning that only changes belief.

**The design implication:** If theory precedes experience, the theory has nothing to attach to. Learners can repeat the words without understanding what they mean. If experience precedes theory, the theory explains something the learner has already lived — and the encoding is incomparably deeper.

**Learning styles** (the contested component): Kolb mapped the four cycle stages onto a two-axis model and described four learning style preferences — Diverger (CE + RO), Assimilator (RO + AC), Converger (AC + AE), Accommodator (AE + CE). People favour different entry points and stages. Teaching that exclusively addresses one stage will reach some learners and miss others.

## The one finding worth quoting in a meeting

*"Learning is the process whereby knowledge is created through the transformation of experience."*

For clinical education: a ward round debrief in which the registrar says aloud what they noticed, how they interpreted it, what they'd do differently, and why — that is Kolb's cycle completed at the bedside, in five minutes, without a room booking.

## Honest limitations

- The Learning Styles Inventory component has been repeatedly and severely critiqued. Learning styles as stable, trait-like individual differences have poor empirical support (Coffield et al., 2004; Pashler et al., 2008). Matching instruction to preferred style does not consistently improve outcomes. The learning styles framing has unfortunately often overshadowed the cycle model in practitioner culture
- The four-stage cycle is a simplification. Real learning does not always proceed through all four stages in sequence, and the stages are not always cleanly distinguishable in practice
- The model was developed and tested primarily in managerial and professional education contexts; it is not derived from classroom or clinical research
- The emphasis on individual experience and reflection can understate the social and situated dimensions of learning — addressed more fully by Vygotsky (1978) and Lave & Wenger (1991)
- "Reflection" as a stage is easier to prescribe than to operationalise — the substantial critical literature on reflective practice (Moon, 1999; Boud, 2001) extends well beyond what Kolb's model specifies

## What it means for your practice

One structural change that applies to any session you design:

**Sequence as experience → reflection → theory → application — not the other way round.** Before you teach the principle, give participants an experience that will need the principle to explain it. The bad slide in Session 1 comes before Sweller. The forgetting word cloud in Session 2 comes before Ebbinghaus. The retrieval quiz in Session 3 comes before the discussion of retrieval practice. None of this is accidental.

If you remove the experiential component of a session you're designing and just give the theory directly, ask: what is lost? Usually the honest answer is that nothing much is gained by the theory without the experience first — learners can receive it but they cannot yet use it.

## How it appears in Teaching That Sticks

The architecture of all three sessions follows Kolb's cycle. Each session opens with a concrete experience (the bad slide demonstration, the forgetting word cloud, the retrieval quiz), moves to brief reflective observation (what happened? what do you notice?), introduces the theoretical framework to explain it, then requires participants to apply it in their own context. The facilitator notes explicitly flag this sequence — *"theory follows experience, not the other way round"* — and instruct facilitators to hold the explanation until after the experience has landed.
