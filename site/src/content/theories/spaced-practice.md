Ebbinghaus mapped the forgetting curve in 1885 and the finding has held ever since: memory decays steeply after initial learning, and the decay accelerates unless the material is revisited. The antidote is not more intensive initial study — it's *distribution*. Learning the same material across multiple sessions, with gaps between them, produces dramatically stronger long-term retention than the same total study time massed together.

The mechanism involves sleep and memory consolidation, synaptic strengthening, and the reconstruction effect of retrieval practice. When you return to something after a gap — just before it would be forgotten — the act of re-accessing it strengthens the trace and extends the next forgetting curve. Space it correctly and you can achieve durable retention with surprisingly little total study time.

The problem for clinical education is structural: most teaching is episodic. A session happens, attendees leave, and the content is never revisited in a deliberate way. That's massed practice by default.

## What this means for teaching

- **Revisit deliberately** — build retrieval of previous session content into the opening of every new one. Not a summary (passive) but a recall task (active).
- **Teach less, return more** — covering 10 topics once produces less retention than covering 5 topics twice, with a gap between exposures. Ruthless curation enables spaced return.
- **Exploit the forgetting curve** — the optimal moment to revisit is when forgetting has begun but the memory is still retrievable. For a 90-minute clinical session, a brief retrieval prompt 24–48 hours later (a message, a follow-up question, a reflection prompt) meaningfully extends retention.
- **Design for return** — if the programme has multiple sessions, use early ones as content to retrieve in later ones. The sessions themselves are spaced practice for their own material.
