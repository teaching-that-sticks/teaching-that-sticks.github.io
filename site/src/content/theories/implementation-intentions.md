Most professional development ends with good intentions. Participants leave energised, intend to change something, and then don't — not because they lack motivation, but because intention without implementation structure evaporates on contact with the actual working day.

Peter Gollwitzer's research showed something simple and powerful: if you link an intention to a specific situation — *when I see X, I will do Y* — follow-through rates roughly double. The situational cue acts as an automatic trigger, bypassing the need to remember and decide in the moment.

This is not a motivational technique. It's a cognitive one. Implementation intentions work by creating a mental association between a context and a response. When the context appears, the response fires without deliberate effort. Which is precisely what you need when you're mid-ward-round, mid-consultation, or mid-whatever-clinical-reality-looks-like-that-day.

## What this means for teaching

- **End every session with a plan, not a reflection** — "What will you do differently?" is weaker than "When will you next teach something? What is one thing you'll change? What will prompt you to do it?" The specificity is the mechanism.
- **If-then prompts are a closing activity** — "If I'm preparing a teaching session and I notice I have more than five slides, then I will..." takes three minutes to write and significantly increases the probability of the behaviour.
- **Intentions decay; plans persist** — the learning from a session is most at risk in the first 72 hours, before new habits compete with old ones. An implementation plan is protective.
- **The clinical environment is full of cues** — bedside, ward round, handover, debrief. Helping participants identify *when* and *where* they'll apply new approaches is more useful than exhortation to apply them generally.
