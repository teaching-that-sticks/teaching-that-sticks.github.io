Donald Kirkpatrick's four-level evaluation model asks the question most training programmes avoid: did it actually make any difference?

The four levels move from easy to hard. **Level 1 (Reaction):** did participants enjoy it and find it relevant? Easy to measure; correlates weakly with learning. **Level 2 (Learning):** did knowledge, skills, or attitudes change? Harder to measure; more meaningful. **Level 3 (Behaviour):** did participants change what they do back in the workplace? Hard to measure; this is the actual goal of most professional development. **Level 4 (Results):** did the organisation benefit? Very hard to measure; rarely attempted.

Most NHS training is evaluated at Level 1 — the post-course happy sheet. Positive reactions feel good but don't predict behaviour change. The gap between Level 2 and Level 3 is where most professional development quietly fails: people learn the thing in the room, intend to apply it, and then don't. Path analyses in clinical education confirm this transition "rarely occurs automatically" — it requires deliberate structural support that most programmes never provide.

The **New World Kirkpatrick Model** (Kirkpatrick & Kirkpatrick, 2016) names what that support requires: *Required Drivers* — the environmental conditions that reinforce training back in the workplace. Research in clinical education identifies **psychological safety** as a primary Required Driver: if learners return to hierarchical environments where they cannot safely try new approaches, Level 2 learning will not become Level 3 behaviour. This is why the psychological safety work in Session 2 is not a detour — it is a structural prerequisite for Session 3 to be worth running.

## What this means for teaching

- **Design for Level 3 from the start** — behaviour change doesn't happen because learning happened. It requires deliberate transfer support: follow-up, implementation planning, peer accountability, managerial reinforcement. If none of those exist, expect Level 2 to be the ceiling.
- **Happy sheets are not evaluation** — participant satisfaction is useful feedback for the facilitator, but it is not evidence that the training worked. An engaging session can produce Level 1 without Level 2. A difficult one can produce Level 3 without Level 1. 
  
  There is an important corollary for active learning: retrieval practice, think-pair-share, and design challenges require genuine cognitive effort, which participants can experience as difficulty. Active sessions frequently produce *lower* Level 1 scores than passive ones, even when durable retention is significantly higher. If you run Session 1 or 2 of this programme and Level 1 scores drop compared to a conventional lecture, that is not evidence the session failed — it may be evidence it worked harder.
- **Name the levels with your group** — in Session 3, we ask participants to evaluate their own previous training using the Kirkpatrick lens. The recognition that "we've all sat through Level 1 training sold as Level 3 training" is itself a motivation to design differently.
- **Plan the 30-day follow-up** — if you don't have a mechanism to ask "did you actually change your teaching?" 30 days after the session, you are choosing to stay at Level 2. That choice has consequences for learners.
