John Biggs noticed something obvious once you see it: students learn what they think they're going to be assessed on, not what the teacher thinks they're teaching.

If your learning outcome says "critically evaluate" but your activity is a 45-minute lecture and your assessment is a multiple-choice quiz — you haven't taught critical evaluation. You've taught recall. The system is *aligned*, just not towards the thing you wanted.

Constructive alignment has three moving parts. The **intended learning outcome** defines what a student should be able to *do* with the knowledge — not "understand" (which means nothing) but "explain", "apply", "diagnose", "design". The **teaching and learning activity** gives students practice doing that thing. The **assessment task** asks them to demonstrate they can do it.

## What this means for teaching

- **Start with the verb, not the noun** — "Understand fluid balance" is not an outcome. "Explain to a patient why their legs are swollen" is an outcome. The verb tells you what the activity and assessment need to look like.
- **Work backwards** — design the assessment first. What would you accept as evidence that a student has learned what you intend? Now build the activity that develops that capacity.
- **Misalignment is invisible to the teacher** — it only shows up in assessment results, or when a trained clinician can't do the thing they were taught. Audit your own teaching: do the activities actually require what the outcome demands?
- **Alignment isn't bureaucracy** — the framework is a thinking tool, not a form to fill in. The question "does this activity develop what I'm assessing?" takes thirty seconds to ask and can reframe an entire session.
