Re-reading notes feels productive. Highlighting feels productive. Going over something again until it feels familiar feels productive. None of these strategies reliably produce durable learning. The research on retrieval practice shows that the act of *pulling information out of memory* — not putting it in — is what makes it stick.

Every time you retrieve a memory, you reconstruct it. That reconstruction strengthens the memory trace, making it easier to retrieve next time and more resistant to forgetting. Passive re-exposure does nothing comparable. The "testing effect" is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology, and it transfers to real educational settings, including medicine.

The implication is uncomfortable: teaching that maximises understanding in the room minimises retention over time, because understanding comes from clear presentation, but retention comes from effortful retrieval. A student who leaves feeling they understood everything may retain less than one who was asked to struggle to recall it.

## What this means for teaching

- **Start with a retrieval quiz, not a recap** — "Before we start, write down everything you can remember from last session" works better than a slide summary. The effort of recall — even unsuccessful recall — primes learning.
- **Low-stakes tests are teaching activities** — a quick written or verbal recall exercise at the end of a session strengthens the memory traces formed during it. This is not assessment; it's consolidation.
- **Avoid over-explaining** — when a student gives a partial answer, resist the urge to complete it. The moment of retrieval struggle is the learning. Prompt and wait.
- **Space the questions** — retrieval works best when there's a delay between learning and retrieval. A question about last week's session at the start of this one is more powerful than a question about this session at the end of it.
